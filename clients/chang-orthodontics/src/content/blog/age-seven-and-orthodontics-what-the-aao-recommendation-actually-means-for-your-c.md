If your child is around six or seven years old, you may have heard that this is the age when an orthodontist should take a first look—even if their teeth look perfectly fine to you. That recommendation comes from the American Association of Orthodontists (AAO), and it's worth understanding what it actually means before you either panic or dismiss it. The short answer: an evaluation at age seven is not a prediction that your child needs braces right now. It's a check to see whether anything is developing in a way that would be easier—and less expensive—to address early rather than waiting until all the permanent teeth are in.

Most children who come in for an age-seven evaluation leave without any active treatment scheduled. The visit gives Dr. Russell Chang a chance to look at jaw development, the relationship between the upper and lower arches, and whether the mix of baby and permanent teeth is progressing normally. For the small percentage of children where something does need attention, catching it at this stage can mean the difference between a straightforward interceptive appliance now and more complex treatment—possibly including extractions or jaw surgery—later.

## Why Age Seven? The Developmental Timing Behind the Recommendation

By around age seven, most children have their first permanent molars and at least some of their permanent front teeth. This combination is important. The molars establish the back of the bite—what orthodontists call the "posterior occlusion"—while the front teeth reveal how the upper and lower jaws are relating to each other. With both pieces in place, a trained orthodontist can evaluate the bite in three dimensions: front to back, side to side, and vertically.

Before age seven, there simply isn't enough permanent dentition to make a reliable assessment of how things are tracking. After the late elementary years, some skeletal patterns become harder to redirect because jaw growth has progressed too far. Age seven sits in a window where problems can be identified while the child's growth is still a tool the orthodontist can use rather than something that has already locked in a problem.

It's also worth noting that the AAO recommendation is for an *evaluation*, not an automatic course of treatment. An orthodontist who sees every seven-year-old and recommends treatment for every seven-year-old is doing something very different from what the AAO intends.

## What an Early Orthodontic Evaluation Actually Looks At

During an evaluation at this age, Dr. Chang will typically take a clinical look at your child's teeth and bite, and in many cases a panoramic X-ray or a cephalometric (side-view) X-ray will be taken. These images show what's happening beneath the gum line: whether permanent teeth are developing, whether they're positioned to erupt normally, and how the jawbones themselves are shaped and aligned.

Specific things the evaluation screens for include:

- **Crossbites** — When upper teeth sit inside lower teeth, either in the front or the back. Front crossbites and posterior crossbites can both affect jaw development if left alone during growth years.
- **Underbites** — When the lower jaw protrudes forward relative to the upper. This is one of the patterns most reliably improved by early treatment because the window for guiding jaw growth is finite.
- **Severe crowding** — In some cases, early assessment of crowding can inform a decision about whether a baby tooth should be held in place or strategically removed to guide incoming permanent teeth.
- **Habits affecting jaw development** — Prolonged thumb-sucking or pacifier use beyond age four can create an open bite (where front teeth don't touch when the back teeth are closed) or a narrowed upper arch. An early visit gives parents concrete information about whether a habit has caused structural change and what, if anything, should be done.
- **Missing or extra teeth** — Panoramic X-rays taken at this age frequently reveal teeth that are present in the bone but haven't erupted yet, as well as supernumerary (extra) teeth that could block normal eruption.

If none of these issues are present, the recommendation is typically to monitor and return for periodic check-ins—often annually—until the child's permanent teeth have fully come in, at which point a more complete orthodontic assessment is done.

## Phase One vs. Phase Two Treatment: What the Terms Mean

When early treatment is recommended, it's usually called Phase One, or interceptive treatment. This is distinct from comprehensive orthodontic treatment, which is sometimes called Phase Two and typically begins after most or all of the permanent teeth have erupted (usually around ages 11–13).

Phase One treatment isn't always—or even usually—braces. Depending on what's been identified, it might involve a palatal expander to widen a narrow upper arch, a space maintainer to hold room for an incoming permanent tooth, or a habit appliance to help a child break a thumb-sucking pattern. Treatment at this stage is generally shorter in duration than full braces—often six to twelve months—and is focused on one or two specific problems rather than final tooth alignment.

A common question parents ask is whether Phase One treatment means their child will definitely need Phase Two treatment afterward. The honest answer: it depends. Phase One addresses specific skeletal and developmental problems. It does not eliminate the need for braces to address final tooth alignment in most cases, but it can make that later treatment simpler, shorter, and potentially less invasive. In some cases—particularly with crossbites and underbites caught early—Phase One treatment achieves results that would have required surgery if treatment had been delayed.

Phase Two, when it happens, looks like standard orthodontic treatment: full brackets or clear aligners to bring all the permanent teeth into their ideal positions.

## FAQ

**Does every child need orthodontic treatment at age seven?**
No. The majority of children who are evaluated at age seven don't need any active treatment at that time. The evaluation exists to identify the minority of children for whom early treatment would make a meaningful difference.

**Will my insurance cover an early orthodontic evaluation?**
Coverage varies by plan. Many dental insurance plans with an orthodontic benefit cover an initial orthodontic consultation. It's worth calling your insurer before the visit to ask specifically about coverage for an orthodontic evaluation versus treatment.

**If my child needs Phase One treatment, does that mean they'll skip braces later?**
Usually not. Phase One addresses specific developmental problems—jaw width, bite direction, space maintenance. It doesn't typically replace the full alignment work that comprehensive orthodontic treatment accomplishes. Think of Phase One as solving a structural problem so that Phase Two, if needed, can go more smoothly.

**My child is eight or nine. Did we miss the window?**
Not necessarily. The age-seven recommendation exists because seven is the earliest point at which a useful evaluation can usually be done—not because problems disappear if you wait a year or two. Bring your child in; the appropriate recommendation will be based on their specific development, not their age alone.

**What's the difference between a pediatric dentist referral and going directly to an orthodontist?**
A pediatric dentist can identify many developing issues and will often refer to an orthodontist when something warrants a specialist's eye. An orthodontist has additional post-doctoral training specifically in jaw development and tooth movement, so the early evaluation is typically more detailed when done in an orthodontic setting.
