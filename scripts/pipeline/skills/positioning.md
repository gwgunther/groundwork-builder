# Positioning Skill

This document defines how to assess a dental practice's signals and translate them into a sharp, specific positioning recommendation. It feeds into the Site Audit step (Phase 2b), whose output drives both design and copy. If this recommendation is generic, everything downstream will be generic.

---

## The Job

Positioning answers one question: **why would a patient in this city choose this practice over any other?**

A good positioning recommendation gives copy and design a specific angle to work from. A bad one is a list of adjectives that could describe any dental office in the country.

**Weak:** "Warm, professional neighborhood dental practice focused on patient comfort."
**Strong:** "The go-to cosmetic dentist for young professionals in a rapidly gentrifying neighborhood — modern, aesthetic-forward, and approachable without being discount."

---

## How to Read the Signals

Before recommending positioning, assess what the scraped data actually reveals:

**Service mix tells you the real business.** A practice with implants, Invisalign, veneers, and whitening is a cosmetic-leaning practice regardless of what their homepage says. A practice with preventive, pediatric, and family services is a family anchor. Let the services reveal the positioning, not just the marketing copy.

**Doctor bio and credentials reveal differentiation.** Advanced training in implants, sleep dentistry, or orthodontics is a genuine differentiator. A generic "Dr. X is committed to continuing education" is not. Only surface credentials that are specific and verifiable.

**Testimonials reveal what patients actually value.** If every review mentions how painless or gentle the experience was, anxiety/comfort is the real differentiator — even if the practice markets on technology. Lead with what patients confirm.

**Location context matters.** A practice in a dense urban neighborhood has different positioning pressure than a suburban strip mall. If the city or neighborhood has a recognizable character (young families, affluent suburb, working-class area), the positioning should reflect that audience.

**Years in practice + patient volume signal trust.** A practice that's been open 15+ years in the same location has community trust as a real asset. A newer practice needs to differentiate on something else — the doctor's training, a specific service, a modern experience.

## Positioning Archetypes

Use these as starting points, not boxes to force practices into. Most practices are a blend.

**The Neighborhood Anchor**
Long-established, multi-generational patient base, strong on family and preventive. Positioning: trust, continuity, community. Works when: the practice has years in the area and testimonials from long-term patients.

**The Cosmetic Specialist**
Veneers, whitening, Invisalign, smile makeovers are prominent. Patients are aesthetic-motivated. Positioning: transformation, confidence, modern aesthetics. Works when: cosmetic services dominate the service mix and the doctor has notable training or cases.

**The Modern Experience**
Technology-forward (CEREC, digital X-rays, 3D imaging), contemporary office design, online booking, minimal wait times. Positioning: convenience, modernity, efficiency. Works when: the existing site or testimonials emphasize technology or the patient experience over specific services.

**The Comfort Practice**
Leads with anxiety and pain messaging — sedation dentistry, gentle approach, accommodating patients who avoid the dentist. Positioning: safety, gentleness, non-judgment. Works when: testimonials repeatedly mention fear or avoidance, or sedation is offered.

**The Comprehensive One-Stop**
Implants, orthodontics, oral surgery, cosmetic — all under one roof. Positioning: convenience, expertise breadth, no-referral experience. Works when: the service list is genuinely broad and the doctor has advanced training across categories.

## Tone Recommendations

Tone should follow positioning logically — don't recommend a tone that contradicts the archetype.

- Neighborhood Anchor → warm, personal, unpretentious
- Cosmetic Specialist → confident, aspirational, polished
- Modern Experience → clean, direct, efficient
- Comfort Practice → gentle, reassuring, non-clinical
- Comprehensive → authoritative, clear, trustworthy

Avoid recommending tones that are vague or universal: "professional and approachable" describes every dental website ever written.

## Differentiators: Real vs. Generic

Only surface differentiators that are **specific and evidenced** by the data.

**Real differentiators:**
- Named advanced training (Kois Center, Las Vegas Institute, implant fellowship)
- A specific service the area likely lacks (full-arch implants in a rural market)
- Unusual hours (evenings/weekends in a commuter market)
- Multi-lingual staff in a market where that matters
- Long tenure in a neighborhood with high turnover (15+ years, same location)
- Specific technology that enables a patient-facing benefit (same-day crowns, no-wait digital X-rays)

**Generic non-differentiators (do not surface these):**
- "Commitment to continuing education"
- "Patient-centered care"
- "State-of-the-art technology" (without naming the technology)
- "Comprehensive dental services"
- "Friendly staff"
- "Accepting new patients"

If the data doesn't support a specific differentiator, say so — don't invent one.

---

## What to Avoid

**Hedging.** Don't recommend a positioning range ("could be warm neighborhood OR modern cosmetic"). Make a call based on the data and explain the rationale. The downstream steps need a clear direction.

**Contradicting the evidence.** If the service mix is overwhelmingly cosmetic but the testimonials are all about gentleness and anxiety, flag the tension — don't just pick one and ignore the other.

**Generic city references.** "Serving the vibrant Long Beach community" is not positioning. Use the city as context, not as the differentiator itself.

**Positioning based on aspirations.** Recommend what the practice *is*, not what they might want to be. A repositioning plan belongs in a strategy engagement, not a website build.
