# Groundwork Copywriting Guidelines

This document defines the voice, tone, and copy standards for all Groundwork-generated dental websites. It is fed directly into the AI content generation step. Update this file to evolve copy quality across all future site builds.

---

## Voice & Tone

**Warm, direct, and grounded.** Write like a trusted friend who happens to be a dentist — not a corporation trying to sound approachable. Confidence without arrogance. Care without sentimentality.

- Warm, not gushing
- Direct, not blunt
- Confident, not boastful
- Local, not try-hard about it

---

## The Cardinal Rule: Patient-First Framing

Every headline, subheadline, and opening line should be written from the patient's perspective — their fear, their goal, their outcome. The doctor and practice exist to serve that story, not to be the hero of it.

**Wrong:** "Dr. Hoang Has Been Taking Care of Long Beach Since 2014."
**Right:** "A Dentist Long Beach Actually Looks Forward to Seeing."

**Wrong:** "We Offer Comprehensive Dental Services."
**Right:** "Everything Your Family's Teeth Need, One Familiar Office."

The patient's desire or problem comes first. The practice is the solution.

---

## Headline Rules

- **5–10 words maximum.** If it needs more, it's not a headline yet.
- **No period on the hero H1.** Periods stop momentum. Use them in body copy only.
- **Lead with benefit, outcome, or feeling** — not with credentials, history, or the doctor's name.
- **Be specific.** "Painless cleanings in 45 minutes" beats "comfortable, efficient care."
- **One idea per headline.** Don't try to say two things.
- **Avoid questions as headlines.** Statements are more confident and convert better.

---

## What to Avoid

**Doctor-as-hero openers.** Headlines that lead with the doctor's name or tenure ("Dr. X has been serving Y for Z years") make the doctor the subject. Patients care about themselves first.

**Vague mission statements.** "We are committed to excellence in dental care" says nothing. Every dentist says this. Skip it.

**Clinical jargon in headlines.** Save "periodontal," "prophylaxis," and "occlusal" for body copy where context makes them appropriate.

**Hype words.** Avoid "cutting-edge," "state-of-the-art," "world-class," "revolutionary." These are self-awarded and empty.

**Passive constructions.** "Smiles are transformed here" → "We transform smiles." Better yet → "You'll leave with a smile you want to show off."

**Fake urgency.** Don't manufacture urgency in CTAs or subheadlines. Dentistry has natural urgency (pain, aesthetics, prevention) — use that.

---

## Hero Section

The hero (above the fold) should do three things in order:

1. **Orient** — Make clear this is a dental practice for people in [City]
2. **Resonate** — Connect with something the patient actually feels (relief, trust, aesthetics, convenience)
3. **Invite** — Give them a clear next step

The tagline (eyebrow text above the H1) can be the location anchor: "Long Beach's Neighborhood Dentist." Let the H1 do the emotional work.

---

## CTAs

- **Primary CTA:** Action + low friction. "Book an Appointment" or "Schedule Online" — clear, no cleverness needed.
- **Secondary CTA:** Exploratory. "See Our Services" or "Meet Dr. [Name]" — for visitors who aren't ready to commit.
- **Avoid:** "Get Started," "Learn More," "Click Here." These are vague and convert poorly.
- **Service page CTAs** should name the service: "Schedule a Cleaning," "Ask About Implants."

---

## Body Copy

- Short paragraphs: 2–3 sentences max.
- One idea per paragraph.
- Write at a 7th–8th grade reading level. Not dumbed down — just clear.
- Use "you" and "your" more than "we" and "our." The patient is the subject.
- Specificity builds trust: name the technology, the process, the time it takes, the outcome.

---

## Local References

- Use the city name naturally — once or twice per page, not obsessively.
- Reference real local context when it's authentic (neighborhood, community ties) — don't manufacture it.
- "Serving Long Beach" is fine. "Proud to serve the vibrant community of Long Beach and its surrounding areas" is not.

---

## Stats & Social Proof

When stats are available (years in practice, patient count, review rating), weave them into body copy as evidence — don't just list them as a badge wall. "Over 1,000 Long Beach families trust us with their dental care" lands harder than a generic "1,000+ Patients" counter.

---

## Services Copy

Each service page intro should:
1. Name who this service is for (implicitly or explicitly)
2. Acknowledge the patient's concern or goal
3. Explain what the practice does and why it works
4. End with a clear CTA tied to that service

Service headlines should be patient-outcome framing, not just service labels:
- "Dental Implants" → "Permanent Teeth That Feel Like Yours"
- "Teeth Whitening" → "A Brighter Smile, Done Right"
- "Emergency Dentistry" → "Same-Day Care When You Need It Most"
